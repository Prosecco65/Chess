Rules of chess!


Originating in India around the 6th century AD, chess came to Europe around the year 1000, thanks to the Arabs.
Chess is played on an 8x8 board with a total of 64 squares on which 16 white pieces and 16 black pieces face off.
The pieces are divided into 8 pawns, 2 rooks, 2 knights, 2 bishops, a queen and a king, each moving differently.
The white pieces start the game.
The pieces can eat the opponent's pieces except for the king, which cannot be eaten.
The goal is to checkmate the king, that is, to block all possible moves.
When the king is in checkmate he is obliged to move.




Pawn


Is the most numerous pawn on the chessboard. During his first movement he can advance two or one square, but from his second movement he can advance only one square per turn.
He can only move forward and can eat the opponent's pieces that are in one of the two oblique squares adjacent to him.
If a pawn manages to reach the opposite side of the board, the pawn's owner must promote it by replacing it with any other piece of his choice (excluding the king).


Rook


They are placed in the corners of the board.
The rook moves both horizontally and vertically by the number of free squares it has available.
It can eat an opponent's piece through occupation of the square where the captured piece is located.
It can be used during castling.


Knight


Knights are placed between the bishops and the rooks.
Each move consists of two steps horizontally followed by one step vertically, so that the path traveled forms an "L."
Is the only piece that can jump other pieces and to eat another piece it must occupy the square where the opponent's piece is located.


Bishop


At the beginning of the game each player has two bishops, one to the left of the queen and one to the right of the king.
The bishop moves diagonally for the number of free squares he has available, the bishop eats an opponent's piece through occupation of the square on which the piece to be captured is located.


Queen


Each player owns one.
The woman can move in straight lines vertically, horizontally or diagonally by the number of unoccupied squares she finds, combining the moves of the rook and bishop.
The woman eats an opponent's piece through occupation of the square on which the piece to be captured is located.


King


Is the most important piece and cannot be eaten.
Whenever the king is threatened with capture, it is said to be "in check," and the defending player is obliged to remedy this situation in the next move.
If it cannot be remedied, it is checkmate and the player loses.
The king may move one square at a time in any direction as long as the target square is not threatened by an opponent's piece.
It can spell the opponent's pieces by moving to the square occupied by the opponent's piece.
Also with the rook he can perform the castling move.


Castling


It consists of moving the king two squares toward a rook on the same rank and then moving the rook to the square that the king passed over.
Castling is permitted only if neither the king nor the rook has previously moved and the squares between the king and the rook are vacant.
